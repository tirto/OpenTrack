I. Executive Summary

The Engineering Computing Systems (ECS) strategic plan is an attempt to set new directions and create strategies for improving information resources and technology support for faculty, staff, and students within the College of Engineering (COE) at San José State University (SJSU).

When Dean Donald Kirk challenged me with the task of creating a "Five Year Plan" for computing, I was at a complete loss as to where I should begin. So in the mood of the times, I turned to the Internet and began searching peer institutions for ideas about how they approached the problem. This report represents the catharsis of my research. Over thirty universities from around the globe are represented in this report. It is therefore understandable that few ideas presented here are original. However, I feel that all the ideas chosen for inclusion are relevant to the College of Engineering and our future.

A special word of thanks is offered to the creators of the Indiana State University, University of Connecticut, University of Missouri, The National University of Singapore, and University of Saskatchewan strategic planning committees, for I have drawn heavily from their respective reports. After reading the reports of these and other peer institutions, several patterns emerged as consistent themes. Naturally, the infrastructure and Internet were common themes. Funding was almost always mentioned, as were concerns about personnel, and the continuing effects technology will have on our future.

Very few reports actually projected their strategies into five year plans. Three years seemed to be the most extreme range of speculation. Many institutions focused on the concept of a "living document" or the adoption of some form of quantifiable yearly progress report. Due to the radical changes already experienced by the growth of technology, the careful step-by-step approaches seem in keeping with the extremely volatile nature of technology. As with most of the reports, I tend to prefer the annual review process as a balanced approach to planning for our future, as opposed to an all encompassing five year plan.

The most important theme that I observed throughout all the papers was the need for increased involvement in information technology at every level of the institution. In the coming years, it is doubtful that few things will affect our lives more than technology; therefore, the greater our participation and planning, the greater our impact on the future.

II. Engineering Computing Systems

The Engineering Computing Systems group is composed of seven full time employees and approximately fifteen student assistants. With some degree of overlap, each full time employee oversees a specific area of expertise such as networking, World Wide Web (WWW) publishing, open student laboratories, research laboratories, desktop support, hardware repair, and administrative systems support. The members of ECS work closely with faculty, staff, and students in the day-to-day operations of the College of Engineering (COE), San José State University (SJSU), and the greater Silicon Valley computer community as a whole.

III. Mission

The mission of ECS is to provide information technology systems, services, computing facilities, and network infrastructure to support the College of Engineering in achieving its academic and administrative missions and goals.

IV. Vision

The vision of ECS is to provide ubiquitous technology-enhanced educational systems and services to all College of Engineering faculty, students, and staff.

To succeed we must build a consensus for our strategic vision. The Dean and Council of Chairs must adopt and commit to our vision for it to succeed. Faculty, staff, and students must be included in the process at every level of creation and implementation.

A strategic information technology vision for the College of Engineering is imperative. For without vision, we are not "Engineering A New Millennium."

V. Importance Of Information Resources

Unlike natural resources, information is a resource that is not depleted as it is used. Instead, it increases in value as it is used and re-used to create knowledge.

Information is at the heart of the teaching and learning process and of the research, creative development, and public services. Information technology supplies the tools to build and enhance educational systems.

The application of computers and networks has a positive and multifaceted role in the information revolution. First, computers and networks help manage and store information in ways that allow easy access, retrieval, and manipulation. Second, they increase productivity in the creation of new information. Third, they enable more communication and collaboration among individuals for the purpose of sharing information, thus enabling the creation of new communities of scholars.

The challenge of educational facilities to implement technology has grown exponentially in the past few years. Information technology is now an integral part of every modern educational facility.

Few occurrences point out the importance of information resources more than the upcoming "Year 2000 Bug." Without question, whether any serious damage occurs or not, the event has raised awareness among the general public about the systemic nature of technology and our dependence on it. On New Year's Eve, of the year 2000, most thinking individuals will count down the seconds and wonder, "Will the lights go out?" Technology and information resources now connect everyone on this very small planet.

VI. Relevance of Information Technologies

The computing environment has changed radically during the past five years. No forecasting in the early 1990s, when the Internet began emerging, could have accurately envisioned the all encompassing and global nature of information technology today.

The development trend has been and will likely continue to be smaller, faster, and cheaper computers, workstations, and personal devices. The cost of computers relative to their performance has dropped drastically. It is expected that this trend will continue, and predictions are that capacity for processing power of computer chips in the next five years will exceed the capacity of today's PowerPC, Pentium, and Merced chips by many times, along with a large corresponding increase in memory. Such systems, along with advanced networking capacity, will offer new opportunities to support teaching, learning, research, and the day-to-day operations of, including access to and delivery of multimedia databases, video conferencing, imaging, and complex simulation and modeling databases. Never has the future of pedagogy been brighter.

Computers, networking, and telephone technologies increasingly will be used to deliver distance learning courses both as a supplement to and, in some cases, a replacement for courses delivered via video. Ongoing demands will outstrip capacity within the next few years (if not already), particularly for multimedia data, document imaging, audio/video conferencing, and increased use of the Internet. Installation of additional fiber-optic broadband and ATM (asynchronous transfer mode) networks currently are the likely directions for increasing capacity. Nonetheless, we are in the beginning of the information age. The advances made in personal computers in the last ten years will continue to be reflected in networking and the interconnectivity of personal devices in the future.

Software continues to become increasingly user friendly. The learning curve once associated with computing on the desktop has all but disappeared. Today, technology has advanced to the point of waiting for us, rather than us waiting for it. Today, creativity in our use of technology is a greater factor than the ability of technology to produce results.

VII. Planning Assumptions

Planning for the future is a daunting task, especially in the field of information technology. However, we must plan; for not to plan is the worst mistake we can make. The following list contains some of the more commonly recognized factors that can affect strategic planning.

  • Costs will always be an inhibiting factor.

  • Many students will enter the College of Engineering with increasing expectations and sophistication about accessing information resources and technology in support of learning.

  • Increasing needs and expectations of the workplace will require college graduates to be knowledgeable in the use of information resources, computers, and networks.

  • Faculty requests will increase for access to and support of information resources and technologies for teaching and research.

  • The need to upgrade, replace, and enhance computer hardware and software, networks, and information resources will be ongoing.

  • More often, Engineering Computing Systems will be called upon to offer consultation, training, and services and to assist faculty to integrate information resources and technologies into instruction.

  • There will be increased use of enhanced multimedia capabilities merging voice, full-motion video, still visuals, and text to support instruction in the classroom.

  • Pressure will continue to grow from the private sector to hire skilled personnel from the university environment with higher paying jobs and benefits.

  • Funding from the State of California will continue to be uncertain. Thus, partnerships with off-campus entities and external funding will become increasingly important.

  • Training in order to stay abreast of technological changes will become more necessary.

  • Service centers are traditionally under-funded relative to demand.

  • It is unlikely that we can increase our tuition rates sufficiently to cover new costs.

  • We are not likely to be successful in implementing a "technology fee."

  • Currently there appears to be little support for a "lease to buy" laptop program. If such a program were adopted, it should probably originate from the CIO's office and be voluntarily implemented at the college level.

VIII. Engineering Computing System's Strategic Plan

Ideally, a strategic plan should define a course of action. In this instance, many courses of action are proposed to achieve our vision. The primary intent of this document is not to present a map to the future, but rather to stir discussion about where we are technologically and where we want to be in one-to-five years. The following goals are offered in keeping with our vision of providing "ubiquitous technology-enhanced educational systems and services." In essence, our plan is to pursue our goals until our vision of the future is a reality. With the help and input of the combined College of Engineering faculty, staff, and students we shall define a course of action in keeping with our vision. The following goals are suggestions for achieving our vision:

  • Develop a coherent planning, evaluating, and budgeting process to help define and improve services offered to students, faculty, administrators, and staff.

  • Develop funding strategies in conjunction with the Dean's Office of Development as part of the base budget for continual upgrading and replacement of information resources and technologies and their ongoing maintenance.

  • Provide and promote a networking infrastructure to support instruction, research, and the day-to-day operations of the College of Engineering.

  • Maintain and enhance a support infrastructure to enable student assistants to acquire general and discipline-oriented knowledge of information resources and technologies.

  • Work with academic and administrative departments to develop strategies that will improve support for consultation and training especially in the area of the World Wide Web and multimedia publishing.

  • Create and maintain a data warehouse for administrative management information that can be used to enhance decision making.

  • Adopt "Open Standards" and "Open Source" code in software and avoid proprietary systems.

  • Build and maintain a service request center complete with job tracking, performance evaluation, and analysis capability to enhance service and productivity.

  • Support faculty in their teaching and research responsibilities.

  • Ensure that all users, including those with disabilities, have equal access to computing and information resources.

  • Encourage and support the staff of ECS in their continuing professional development and accreditation.

  • Improve the ability to access institutional administrative files by students, faculty, and administrators to improve advising.

  • Coordinate our efforts with the overall University Strategic Plan of the Chief Information Officer, as well as promote effective communication between Engineering Computing Systems and other campus constituencies.

  • Increase the COE capacity for accessing the Internet by adding new hardware and software, thus increasing its bandwidth to support multimedia applications.

  • Review and upgrade the existing network infrastructure.

  • Continue to investigate the feasibility of wireless communication.

  • Prepare for any "Year 2000 Bug" aberrations by coordinating with the central campus computing facilities and physical plant departments.

  • Determine, with advice from the user community, the service and support that should be provided with available resources.

  • Involve students, faculty, administrators, and staff, in the planning, decision making and implementation of services provided by Engineering Computing Systems.

  • Promote a service attitude based on a firm commitment to excellence, quality, and consistency.

  • Annually, coordinate strategic plans for each of the College of Engineering departments based on their needs and in support of Engineering Computing Systems and the University strategic plans.

IX. What We Can Do Now

  • Aggressively seek funding.

  • Strive for excellence.

  • In conjunction with the Information Technology Committee (ITC), present the Dean and Council of Chairs a comprehensive strategic vision for academic and administrative computing and obtain their support.

  • Enhance the research support environment by coordinating academic and Engineering Computing Systems. In particular, "Senior Projects" could be enhanced by coordination with Engineering Computing Systems support staff.

  • Continue to improve classroom, laboratory, and support facilities for learning by coordinating efforts between departments through the Information Technology Committee.

  • Establish the multimedia (voice, data, and video) infrastructure.

  • Analyze other efforts in peer higher education by establishing contacts, both in person and via electronic means.

  • Faculty and staff efforts to transform teaching, learning, research and service must be recognized and rewarded.

  • Build an inexpensive Linux-clustered supercomputer.

  • Ensure hardware and software maintenance of advanced computer systems once they are purchased and installed such as the UNIX workstations used in Computer Information and Systems Engineering (CISE) and Electrical Engineering (EE).

  • Support continuous diffusion of technology information among faculty and staff by providing a Web page devoted to new technologies and virus updates.

  • Consider how the use of IT might alter the use of physical space. In particular, the class scheduling program currently being written and tested.

  • Recognize that staffing levels must be continuously reviewed.

  • Address security of the physical space and computing environment in particular.

  • Add a full time employee who is dedicated to supporting Microsoft products.

  • Propose an action-oriented agenda with a coherent theme such as, "Engineering A New Millennium."

  • Establish a "Disaster Recovery Plan" for essential data and records.

  • Develop or adapt interfaces that are user-friendly, transparent, seamless, powerful, and flexible. For instance, a standardized Internet/Intranet graphic user interface supported over multiple platforms for ease of use by anyone, anywhere.

  • Enhance and expand the functionality of the existing "Document Center."

  • Standardize data definitions to be used in the data warehouse central repository.

  • Determine and enforce standards for integrity and quality of the data in the data warehouse.

  • Develop a basic core set of data inquiries to allow people timely and effective access to information.

  • Adopt electronic mail as the communication form of choice.

  • Encourage imaging and electronic publishing to reduce paper usage.

  • Establish evaluation methods and processes at the inception of programs to provide a mechanism for continual adjustment and improvement.

  • Determine the current situation perceptions of information technology via a poll or Web based survey.

  • Update and train constituencies as to the state of the art in security and encryption. In particular, our existing Secure Socket Level (SSL) technology and virus protection software should be fully documented and explained to users for the purpose of developing E-commerce applications.

  • Provide education about the responsibility and ethics of confidentiality.

  • Identify a central authority to coordinate acquisition of hardware and software to ensure compatibility with our system and reduce costs.

  • Place greater emphasis on the Information Technology Committee (ITC) and increase its membership to contain at least one Chairperson and one student representative.

X. The Benefits Are

  • Faculty will receive the encouragement, incentives, and support they need to improve teaching and research.

  • A growing number of faculty in many disciplines will find that computer-managed information, especially multimedia, will support more effective approaches to their instruction.

  • Computers, personal devices, and networking developments will continue their convergence to provide a very powerful information access, retrieval, management, and distribution tool of both scholarly information and information from administrative files in a timely and accurate fashion.

  • Information resources and technology increasingly will be built into the curriculum design to enhance teaching and learning and to provide better ways for students and faculty to communicate, collaborate, and experiment.

  • Through a state-of-the-art network, students, faculty, administrators, and staff will have universal access to distributed information and multimedia applications.

  • A consultation and training support system provided by ECS will provide guidance in the use of information resources and technologies with special attention on how they can be incorporated into the curriculum.

  • Many of the current inconveniences of accessing campus resources that face distance learners and persons with disabilities will be diminished through computer technology, networks, and information in electronic formats.

  • A data warehouse will assist in class scheduling, advising, productivity increases, and better decisions due to readily available and reliable data.

  • The use of email and imaging systems will continue to reduce the need for paper-based information in many areas of the College of Engineering and will increase productivity.

  • Funding and budgeting will become more stable thereby allowing for systematic replacement of outdated and expensive equipment with up-to-date reliable systems.

  • Faculty will be more effective because they have continued to master new technologies.

  • Faculty and student retention will improve.

  • The public image of the College of Engineering will be enhanced which should lead to more private, corporate, and national grants.

  • The College of Engineering will continue to experience information technology related productivity increases. Improved communication will be facilitated through increased use of email, imaging, Internet, and Intranet publishing.

  • With strong and successful implementation of information technology, the College of Engineering will be competitive in attracting quality students and, in the emerging Internet 2 environment, will be successful in securing competitive research funding.

  • The College of Engineering can become a model for the University and perhaps the entire California State University system.

  • With a clear vision, every faculty, staff, and student will know what we did and why we did it.

XI. What Can Go Wrong

  • If we are to succeed, a source of funds must be identified. Funding is a key element if significant advances are to be made in the University's strategic use of IT.

  • The lack of endorsement at the Dean and Council of Chairs level.

  • Plans must be capable of being implemented and sustained.

  • Plans must realistically reflect the COE's resources.

  • Plans and programs must be focused and prioritized.

  • The lack of appropriate motivation and rewards for IT integration.

  • Expectations beyond our resources.

  • The pace of technological change.

  • Political shifts at both the university, state, and national level.

  • Continue without a plan.

The biggest risk is doing nothing and hoping the future will take care of itself. In the final analysis, doing nothing is the most dangerous approach The current system of "act-react" or "fire-fighting" cannot sustain us into the future. We must develop and adopt a realistic plan and strategy for the future.

XII. Recommendations

  • A detailed analysis of the current information technology environment within the College of Engineering should immediately be undertaken.

  • The Dean and Council of Chairs should embrace a vision for information technology and communicate this vision as a major College of Engineering policy.

  • Following upon the communication of a College of Engineering information technology strategy, academic Chairs should be instructed to develop departmental academic plans that set forth goals for the integration of technology into the teaching, learning, research, and service missions of their individual departments.

  • All College of Engineering departmental strategic plans should be reviewed on a yearly basis by the Information Technology Committee for inclusion into and enhancement of the greater College of Engineering information technology strategic plan.

  • A task force should be appointed to develop and recommend a coordinated funding strategy for information technology within the College of Engineering.
